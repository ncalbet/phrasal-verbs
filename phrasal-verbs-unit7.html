PHRASAL VERBS
A phrasal verb is a verb combined with one or two small words — a preposition or an adverb — that creates a completely new meaning. They are everywhere in everyday English.
Tech
log off · sign out · switch on/off · print out
Social
hang out · meet up with · go away · chill out
Lifestyle
take up · work out
8 slides + quiz
Theory, examples & practice
Verb + particle = new meaning. "Sign out" does not mean "sign" + "out" separately, but forms a phrasal verb meaning "to leave an account or end a session".
Some are separable (object can go in the middle). Some are inseparable (always kept together).

Key Concept
What Is a Phrasal Verb?
The meaning of a phrasal verb is different from the literal meaning of its individual words. You cannot always guess the meaning — you need to learn each one.
Structure
A phrasal verb is formed by:
Verb + particle (adverb or preposition)
Sometimes: Verb + particle + preposition (3 words)
work (verb) + out (adverb) = work out
meet (verb) + up with (particle + prep) = meet up with

Two Types
3 / 8
Grammar
Two Types You Need to Know
All phrasal verbs are either separable or inseparable. This tells you where to place the object.
Type 1 — Separable
The object can go between the verb and the particle, or after the full phrasal verb. Both are correct.
Switch off the laptop.  ✅
Switch the laptop off.  ✅
Pronoun rule — important!
With a pronoun (it, them, her, him), it must go between the verb and the particle. Never after.
Switch it off.  ✅
Switch off it.  ❌
In this unit: switch off/on print out take up log off sign out
Type 2 — Inseparable
The object always comes after the complete phrasal verb. You cannot place anything between the verb and the particle.
Meet up with your friends.  ✅
Meet your friends up with.  ❌
3-word phrasal verbs
Phrasal verbs with three words (verb + adverb + preposition) are always inseparable.
I met up with my friends.  ✅

Technology
Tech & Digital Phrasal Verbs
These four phrasal verbs are all about using technology and devices in daily life. All four are separable.
Separable
Log off
To end a session on a computer, app or website
"Don't forget to log off before you leave the library computer."
"I always log off my school account after class."
Separable
Sign out
To log out of an account or platform — same meaning as log off
"I signed out of Instagram because I needed to focus."
"She signed out of her email on the shared device."
Separable
Switch off / on
To turn a device off / on completely
"Switch off your phone during the exam." → "Switch it off."
"Can you switch on the projector? Switch it on, please."
Separable
Print out
To produce a paper copy of a digital document
"Print out the worksheet before tomorrow's lesson."
"I printed the tickets out this morning." ✅
Log off vs Sign out
Log off is more common with computers and operating systems. Sign out is more common with apps, websites and Google accounts. In practice, they mean the same thing — both are correct.

Social Life
Social & Leisure Phrasal Verbs
These phrasal verbs describe spending time with people and relaxing — very common in everyday conversation. All four are inseparable.
Inseparable
Hang out
To spend time relaxing with friends, with no specific plan
"We're just hanging out at the park after school."
"Do you want to hang out this weekend?"
Inseparable (3 words)
Meet up with
To arrange to see someone at a specific time and place
"I'm going to meet up with my friends at the shopping centre."
"We met up with them at 5 o'clock near the station."
Inseparable
Go away
To travel somewhere and leave your usual place, or to disappear
"We're going away for the weekend — my parents booked a hotel."
"I wish this headache would just go away!"
Inseparable
Chill out
To relax completely and do nothing stressful; to calm down
"After the exam I just want to chill out at home and watch Netflix."
"Chill out! It's not a big deal."
Hang out vs Meet up with
Hang out = informal, no fixed plan, just being together.  Meet up with = arranged meeting at a specific time and place. "We met up with them at 6 and then just hung out all evening."

Lifestyle
Health & Hobbies Phrasal Verbs
Two very useful phrasal verbs about starting new activities and keeping fit.
Separable
Take up
start a new hobby / activity
To start doing a new hobby, sport or activity for the first time
"I've decided to take up photography — I bought a new camera."
"She took up coding last year and now she's amazing at it."
"Have you ever thought about taking up a new sport?"
Separable: "take it up" ✅ — but always needs an object. You cannot say "take up" alone.
Inseparable
Work out
exercise / calculate / solve
To exercise (gym, running…) OR to find a solution / calculate something
"I work out three times a week at the gym."
"I can't work out how to fix this bug in my code."
"We need to work out a solution before the deadline."
Two very different meanings — context makes it clear!

Watch Out!
Common Errors
Can you spot what's wrong before clicking? These are the most frequent mistakes with phrasal verbs.
Error 1
Pronoun after the particle (separable)
❌ "Switch off it when you're done."
✅ "Switch it off when you're done."
With a pronoun (it, them, him, her), it always goes between the verb and the particle. Never after.
Error 2
Splitting an inseparable phrasal verb
❌ "I met my friends up with at the park."
✅ "I met up with my friends at the park."
Meet up with is a 3-word phrasal verb — always inseparable. The object always goes after the full phrasal verb.
Error 3
Confusing log off with switch off
❌ "I logged off the computer." (meaning: turned it off completely)
✅ "I switched off the computer." (to turn off completely)
✅ "I logged off my account." (to end a session)
Log off / sign out = end a user session. Switch off = cut the power completely.
Error 4
Using "take up" without an object
❌ "I want to take up." (no object — what activity?)
✅ "I want to take up skateboarding."
✅ "I want to take up a new hobby."
Take up always needs an object — the activity you are starting. You cannot use it without one.
